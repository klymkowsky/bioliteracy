What is a Biology Concept Inventory (BCI) and how are we building it?

A concept inventory is a multiple choice test to determine what is in the mind of the student; which ideas are accurately understood, which misconceptions are present, and which ideas are missing.  

These are rigorous designed and validated tests, in which the questions and the alternative answers (known as distracters) are based on extensive student interviews. 

 

The distracters are based on commonly held student misconceptions.

As epitomized by the Force Concept Inventory (a test in on the basic ideas of Newtonian mechanics), developed by Hestenes and colleagues, concept inventory tests are powerful tools to bring into focus actual educational outcomes – in physics their use has lead to new approaches to teaching. 

But what about biology?   Biology is an admittedly diverse subject.  How can we even presume to assemble a concept inventory to measure biology learning?  

 
We begin with the premise that learning the basics thoroughly enables students to approach the latest discoveries with confidence and perspective.
 
The faster Science and Technology advance—the more important it is to teach and to learn the basics of Math and Science and the less important it is to teach and to learn the latest developments.
- Harari’s Law of Science education
 

 

So what are these basic concepts that lie at the foundation of biological science?  We have turned our own experiences as research scientists and teachers, as well as the input of teachers around the world, through the Biology Concept Workspace, to assemble a Biology Concept List (BCL).

 

The goal of the BCL is NOT to establish teaching standards.  Rather we are trying to capture what teachers are actually teaching.
 

This establishes a 'bottom-up' consensus of what constitutes biological literacy rather than the more common 'top-down' approach of establishing learning standards.  

It is possible to use the BCL to identify concepts taught in courses and covered in curricula.  

We thank Laura Knoll and the members of the NRC Pilot Workshop on Improving Biology Education for this important insight.
