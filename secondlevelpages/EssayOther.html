Essay questions:

Science and its methods
It is a common misconception that scientific research always produces unambiguous and correct answers; describe what factors can lead to uncertainties in an experiment and describe the methods commonly used to reduce this uncertainty. 
It is common to hear talk about "controls" in scientific experiments. What are "controls" and why are they essential parts of rigorous experiments? 
Biologists refer to both the fact of evolution and to various theories of evolution; what is the difference between a fact and a theory and compare the roles of each in science and non-scientific (for example, religious or philosophical) approaches to the world.
Genetics
You have the same number of chromosomes as your parents.  Half of your chromosomes came from one parent, and the other half from the other parent. Explain how that happens.
Evolutionary mechanisms
In general terms, describe how the appearance of a predator influences the evolution of its prey; what signs in the fossil record would lead you to conclude that the appearance of specific trait was driven by predation? 
There are traits that have no apparent function, for example, sightless eyes in cave animals, the human appendix, pelvic bones in whales and snakes.  How are these traits explained in terms of evolutionary processes?
If evolution leads to organisms that are well adapted to their environment, explain how it is possible that most species that ever lived are now extinct?  (DU4)
Explain why scientists believe that they can learn how species formed in the distant past by investigating how species form today. (DU2)
When scientists classify organism, what criteria do they use and how is evolutionary theory used to resolve issues involving the relationship between organisms?  (DU6)
Sexual reproduction is common among multicellular plants and animals, while asexual reproduction is more common among unicellular organisms.   Describe the selective advantages and disadvantages associated with the evolution of sexual reproduction. (DU3)
Molecular Mechanisms / Molecular Biology
How is genetic information stored in an organism and how it used? 

Cells and their components can be described as molecular machines; how are the activities of these machines controlled?
Cells, Energy and Physiology
Some cells are be organized into tightly adherent sheets, while other cell types behave primarily as distinct individuals.  Describe in general terms, the difference between such cell types.  
Describe how signals impinging on a cell from the outside can lead to a change in the cell’s behavior. 
During embryonic development, and in the adults, there are cells which, when they divide, produce cells with quite different fates.  Explain how it is that a single cell can produce two different types of offspring; include as many methods as you know of.
Developmental Biology / Embryology

Sexual organisms alternate between haploid and diploid phases.  In most animals, the haploid phase is quite transient.  There are, however, certain plants in which there is an extended haploid phase of the life-cycle – explain what benefits are associated with haploid and diploid phases.  (DU5)
During embryonic development, a complex organism forms from a single cell, the fertilized egg.  On the surface, this might seem to violate the laws of thermodynamics, but in fact it does not.  Explain how it is that embryonic development can occur without violating the laws of thermodynamics.  
How are asymmetries established in the early embryo and what role do they play in subsequent developmental events?
During development specific cellular events occur at the appropriate time, sequence and place; explain how a cell knows where, when and how to behave during development.
Describe how it can be that two cells that share the same genetic information can behave quite differently (that is have different shapes, perform different functions, make different proteins).  
In a maternal effect mutation, the genotype of the mother produces a phenotype in the offspring that is not seen in the mother.   Describe, in cellular terms, how this occurs. 
 
Ecology

Explain, in terms of energy sources and flows, why large predators are rarer (in terms of both types and population size) than their prey?

It is common to read that a certain gene is responsible for a certain trait; what exactly does that mean?
Certain ideas, once commonly held to be true, are now no longer seriously discussed by scientists; explain why this is the case and what does it take for a new idea to become commonly accepted within the scientific community. 
What features of cell structure are unique to eukaryotic cells? 
What is the evidence that eukaryotic cells arose through by a symbiotic event involving archaeal and bacterial cells? 
Random events such as genetic drift, founder effects, and bottlenecks can influence evolutionary change in a population.  How does this work, and can these processes produce traits that are not adaptive? 
Spraying with insecticides often does not kill 100% of the target insect population.  Repeated use of the same insecticide over  a long period of time can lead to populations of insects that are resistant to the insecticide.   Describe how such resistant populations appear. 
You had two stacks of agar plates used to grow yeast cells; one stack was placed in the back of the cold room and the other in the front, near the door.  You notice that more of the plates near the front of the cold room were contaminated than were those near the back.  Formulate a hypothesis that might explain this observation and then describe an experiment to test it.
You are discussing gene therapy with a friend and they claim that "it is unnatural, and should be outlawed". Describe what is meant by gene therapy and then why you agree or disagree with your friend's statement.
Describe how one uses a genetic analysis to study a particular cellular or organism process.
There are traits that actually appear to be disadvantageous, such as the bright colors of various insects or the dramatic plumage of certain species of birds; how would you explain the presence of these traits in evolutionary terms?
What is meant by "sexual selection" and how does this process produce traits that might appear, at least superficially, to be disadvantageous to the organism that expresses them.
