Using the Biology Concept List to evaluate course effectiveness.

Finding instruments that effectively and convincingly measure student conceptual understanding can be difficult.  

In the absence of a validated Biology Concept Inventory, we have to look for alternatives. 

One obvious approach is a socratic one, the direct questioning of the student by disinterested experts.  It is this approach that was used by Wright et al. (1998).

There are, however, obvious practical difficulties in this approach; it is expensive in terms of faculty time and energy, particularly in large classes (not to mention that at smaller institutions, it may be difficult to find enough such disinterested "experts").

We have developed an alternative method based on a modified "written interview" approach.  It consists of three parts.  

  • The course designer/instructor selects a set of 10 BCL concept statements that they believe capture important parts of the course – these are ideas that the instructor believes a successful student should be able to work with fluently.    These concept statements will be only a subset of the concepts the course is designed to cover. 
  • This concept statement list is then given to one or two experts in the topics covered; these independent experts design essay questions (3 to 5) that cover a subset of the concept statements; these questions should be opened ended in the sense that complete answers will be expected to require between 100 to 200 words.
  • At the end of the course, each student is given between 2 to 3 of these essay questions to answer.  These answers are given to two independent experts for evaluation.  Before they begin, the experts are asked to meet and develop a rubric that identifies what constitutes a complete and correct answer. 

Answers are evaluated on the basis of the following scale

mastery–partial understanding-weak understanding-misunderstanding-ignorance

and the results reported back to the instructor; although not comprehensive, the analysis has the advantage of being focused on concepts that the instructor themselves identified as critical and can often reveal common misconceptions held by the students.  

 

We have automated this approach using Latent Semantic Analysis, a process developed by Walter Kintsch, Tom Landauer and colleagues.

Our first test of this process indicates that LSA is a viable approach to evaluating such essay type questions.  

We are now assembling a library of such questions that can be used in course assessment.
