.Science education & scientific literacy. 

It seems that every day we are faced with news of scientific breakthroughs, dangers and possibilities. 

A number of reports have identified scientific literacy as a key determinant of national economic well-being and security.  

Yet many evaluations of the publics' "scientific literacy" indicate that most citizens are at best woefully ignorant of, and at worst quite misinformed about the basic principles underlying modern science as well as the basic process by which science works. 

The situation is particularly problematic in the biological sciences, where debates often rage over question of the benefits and risks (pdf) of genetic engineering, stem cells, pesticides, organic foods, irradiation, nutriceuticals, alternative medical therapies, and childhood vaccination to the validity of the theory of evolution.  Sadly, it is all too common that these debates have little anchorage in science or logic. 


"The greatest challenge facing mankind is the challenge of distinguishing reality from fantasy, truth from propaganda." - Michael Crichton

The end result can be flawed, dangerous, misdirected and wasteful decisions, frivolous lawsuits, wasted resources and missed opportunities, as well costly and potentially tragic personal choices.

How is it that our educational system is not better at producing higher, and more common levels of scientific literacy? 

How is it that our educational system is not better at producing higher, and more common levels of scientific literacy? 

An important reason is that the educational system often does not demand that students' attain a facile understanding of the materials presented.

As dramatically illustrated by the video: a private universe, often we are satisfied by what has been termed by McClymer and Knoles (1992) as ersatz learning.   

While the recent embrace of standardized tests as a way of insuring the accountability of the educational system has potential value, it is only as good (or as destructive) as the quality of the exams used. 

There is often a disconnect between content requirements and assessment instruments.  The former tend to be rather over-ambitious in design while the later fall short of measuring conceptual understanding. 

Such inauthentic tests leave both students and instructors with an over-estimation of their students true understanding.  Moreover, without authentic testing as a governor, content is often free to become superficial.  Rigorous and unbiased assessment is a critical "selective pressure" to insure deep and effective learning. 

It is in this light that concept inventories have been particularly important.
